3. Stability analysis of a complex landslide under static and dynamic conditions
A. CANCELLI, Professor of Engineering Geology and G. CROSTA and P. ROMANI, University of
Milan, Italy
The Camporaghena landslide, situated in the Northern Apennines, is a typical example of complex landslide in a high seismicity area. The hillslope stability is controlled by climatic, geological, morphological, structural and geotechnical factors; results of static, pseudostatic and dynamic stability analyses are presented and discussed.
INTRODUCTION
1. This research is part of a multidisciplinary project which has the aim to evaluate the stability of rock slopes in areas of high seismic hazard (CNR-GNDT,
U.R. 1.3.2, University of Milan).
2. The Camporaghena landslide, sited in the North- ern Apennines (Tuscany), was selected for such a purpose. This is the remobilization of a pre-existing old slide (length = 1400 m, breadth = 1000 m, volume about 30 Mm3, surface area = 0.5 km ), in deeply fractured shales and clayey rocks of the
Scaglia Rossa formation, in the form of a complex slide and as more surficial failures which cause evi- dent damage to the village houses.
3. The most recent strong reactivation of the main body of the landslide was caused by the 1920 earthquake (I = 9). High seismicity (average inten- sity = 8) and an annual mean rainfall of 2500 mm/yr in the area make it difficult to assess the stability of the slope. This paper presents the results from an analytical solution for static, pseudostatic and dynamic analysis of the rock slope, on the basis of available macro-seismic, hydrologic, structural and ground-survey data.
GEOLOGIC AND CLIMATIC OUTLINES
4. The Camporaghena landslide is sited on the right- hand side of Taverone torrent, on the apenninic divide between Tuscany and Emily (D'AMATO, 1988), in a highly seismic area.
5. The lithology consists of the non- metamorphic
Tuscan succession (Triassic to Oligocene).
Three geologic formations outcrop in the area: the "Calcari
Cavemosi" formation (limestone and gypsum), the
"Scaglia Rossa" formation (shale and marls) and final- ly the "Macigno" formation (sandstones).
It seems that gypsum strata and diapirism played a leading role for the regional uplift and detachment of huge blocks.
6. The entire slope may be divided in three areas of different mobility. Presently, the western portion is the more active and three main elements can be distin- guished: a major deep-seated old slide (Fig. 1c), some associated translational slides and earthflow and small shallower slides in the toe area. Information on the times, rates and phases of the mass move- ments is incomplete.
7. The seismic history of the area is resumed in a list of the recorded earthquakes, generally with surficial focus, available since 1680. On this base and some other historical data, the map of tectonic flux (Fig. 1a) of the Northern Apenninic area has been generated.
8. Landsliding at Camporaghena, however, is not confined to periods of seismic activity, as it is possible to observe by field and inclinometer measurements.
In fact, this area is subject to periods of heavy rainfall
(max. rainfall intensity = 133 mm/h), as a conse- quence of periodic Tyrrhenian cyclonic perturbances, that can trigger rapid movements of the main slides.
A max rainfall map is produced using 30 yrs records of 16 rain gauge stations (Fig. 1b).
9. As a consequence of the above, a complete hydrological and climatological study was carried out.
GEOTECHNICAL / GEOMECHANICAL PROPERTIES
10. The study of geotechnical properties was carried out on samples of shales from different depths in boreholes (Fig. 1d), coincident with hypothetical slip surfaces. The main soil characteristics, summarized in Table I, are the result of classification and index tests and of some sets of drained direct shear box tests, for a range of normal effective stress up to 300 kPa, on natural and reconstituted samples.
11. The geomechanical characteristics were ex- amined through 30 structural surveying stations
(ISRM standards), distributed both inside and outside the landslide, trying to find a relationship between structures and landslide movements. A statistical analysis was carried out on the data and only the more representative surveys are shown in Fig. 1c.
Finally some laboratory tests were carried out to find bulk density, specific gravity, uniaxial compressive strength (Point Load Strength Test) and the angle of internal friction for rock joints (0 = 35-38°, JRC = 8-
10, according to Barton's, 1971 empirical equation).

13. The first step was to compute the Grandory's damping law for epicentral intensity, on the basis of the well known 1920 earthquake isoseismal lines.
Then Gumbel, Gutemberg and Cornell methods were applied, on a list of events with intensity greater than
6 MCS, to evaluate the recurrence interval. The former was applied to obviate the problem of repeated and precursor events and the second one as a checking test. The last one was employed to take into account the related events. So the explana- tion for the differences, reported in Table II, expecially between Gumbel and Cornell outcome, is the removal from the earthquakes list of repeated events with high intensity.
The results of Cornell technique are, however, in good accordance with a previous analysis (EVA et al, 1986) and so a design recurrence interval of 200 yrs was chosen. The macroseismic in- tensity for such an event is 7.5+8 MCS scale, equal to a 4.85-5.1 magnitude, and the corresponding maxi- mum acceleration (Chiaruttini & Siro eq.) is 0.10-0.14 g. On the basis of these data 5 actual records, each one composed of a couple of strong-motion records for the principal direction (N-S, E-W), with different durations, have been selected (Fig. 2a is an ex- ample), together with one of higher intensity, for New- mark analysis.

STABILITY ANALYSES
14. Different types of stability analyses were con- ducted on the basis of seismic, hydrologic, topographic and inclinometer data. Static (Janbu,
Morgenstern & Price), pseudostatic and dynamic
(NEWMARK, 1965; IGARASHI, 1985; BANDIS, 1988) techniques were used.
15. The main outcome of the static methods (Table
III) was a series of sensitivity tests on the angle of in- ternal friction. Pseudostatic analyses were carried out to obtain the critical acceleration (Table III). The criti- cal acceleration represents the ground motion neces- sary to begin the process of slope failure and then it is the threshold value to exceed for the beginning of the motion. The Newmark analysis, which models the landslide as a rigid friction block resting on a slope, was used to calculate the expected displacements
(Fig. 2b and Table IV for the worst conditions) of the block in the case of the design strong-motion record.
In accordance with IGARASHI (1985) a sensitivity test was employed on a range of critical acceleration values also using strong- motion records of reversed sign. The results are plotted in Fig. 3 which shows the range.
