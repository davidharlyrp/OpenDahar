Technical Note
Effects of Densification and Stiffening on Liquefaction
Risk of Reinforced Soil by Stone Columns
Zeineb Ben Salem, Ph.D.1; Wissem Frikha2; and Mounir Bouassida3
Abstract: The installation of stone columns is widely adopted to prevent liquefaction. The main improvement mechanisms of stone columns as a liquefaction countermeasure are drainage, stiffening, and densification. This paper investigates the effectiveness of stone columns as a liquefaction remediation. Twenty-four case studies, in which SPT and CPT tests are performed before and after stone column reinforcement, are used as a basis of the research. Densification and stiffening mechanisms are investigated and their individual and combined effects are analyzed. Comparative results indicate that considering the densification and stiffening effects considerably improve the assessment of Civil Engineers.
Author keywords: Liquefaction; Stone columns; Densification; Stiffening.
Introduction
Liquefaction has been responsible for tremendous amounts of dam- age in historical earthquakes around the world. The liquefaction occurs when excess pore pressure increases to the point where the effective stress and shear resistance of the soil become very low, leading to severe loss of strength and stiffness (Marcuson
1978). Several approaches have been developed over the years to evaluate the liquefaction potential. Seed and Idriss (1971) ini- tially proposed a procedure known as the simplified procedure.
This procedure compares the cyclic shear stress ratio (CSR) caused by the design earthquake with soil’s liquefaction resistance ex- pressed in terms of cyclic resistance ratio (CRR). This procedure has been modified and improved, e.g., by Cetin et al. (2004) and
Idriss and Boulanger (2008) based on several field test results in- cluding the standard penetration test (SPT) and the cone penetration test (CPT). To reduce the risk of liquefaction potential and its as- sociated damages, various ground improvement methods are used, including densification, reinforcement, grouting/mixing and drain- age. Reinforcement with granular columns is the most widely adopted method to mitigate liquefaction (Mitchell and Wentz
1991). Extensive research has been conducted to assess the effec- tiveness of stone columns in mitigating liquefaction (Boulanger et al. 1998; Adalier et al. 2003; Rollins et al. 2009). This reinforce- ment can mitigate the risk of liquefaction through several mecha- nisms by (1) providing drainage which dissipates the induced excess pore-water pressure almost as fast as it is generated;
(2) introducing stiff elements which potentially can carry higher stress levels, causing a reduction in the stress levels in the surround- ing soil; and (3) increasing the soil density during installation pro- cess of stone columns (Ben Salem et al. 2016).
This paper assesses the effectiveness of stone columns as a liquefaction countermeasure, focusing on densification and stiffen- ing mechanisms. The analysis is performed using a database col- lected from 24 cases studies in which SPTs and CPTs were undertaken before and after stone column installation.

The CSR was defined using the procedure of Seed and Idriss

Analysis of Liquefaction Potential of Reinforced Soil
To analyze the liquefaction potential of reinforced soil, the densi- fication improvement was evaluated using SPTs and CPTs performed before and after stone column installation. The stiffening effects were analyzed through several approaches from the literature.
Densification Effect
The SPT and CPT results were corrected. Figs. 1(a and b) report comparative results of initial and final penetration resistance
Ncorrected and qcorrected values, respectively, recorded before and after stone columns installation. Penetration resistance greatly increased after stone column installation. Significant ground densification was achieved in clean to slightly silty sands, whereas densification of the silt soils was very limited. In sensitive clay layers, penetra- tion resistance decreased. The increase in the penetration resistan- ces Ncorrected and qcorrected ranged from 31.13 to 69.29% and from
57.10 to 318.28%, respectively. The amount of improvement can be attributed to the installation of the stone columns.
Factors which influence the effectiveness of the soil densifica- tion after stone column installation include the soil type, predensi- fication relative density, vibratory type, area replacement ratio, and spacing between stone columns.
Stiffening Effect
It has been suggested that stone columns, which have high shear modulus relative to the surrounding soils, potentially can carry higher shear stress levels. Thus stone columns provide shear reinforcement and decrease the CSR to which the treated soils would be subjected during an earthquake (Rayamajhi et al.
2014). This stiffening mechanism is considered to be more effective for silt and clayey layers in which densification and drainage are difficult to achieve. This is based on the assumption that stiff col- umns deform compatibly in shear with the surrounding soil, as re- ported by Baez and Martin (1993) and Priebe (1998). However, other studies have concluded that stone columns may deform in both flexure and shear such that they are less effective in reducing shear stresses than the assumption of shear strain compatibility im- plies (Goughnour and Pestana 1998; Green et al. 2008; Olgun and
Martin 2008; Rayamajhi et al. 2014). Assuming shear strain com- patibility, the benefits of reducing shear stress in reinforced soil are overestimated (Rayamajhi et al. 2014). In order to quantify the level of shear stress reduction in reinforced soil, several expressions have been developed to estimate the shear stress reduction factor KG, defined as
KG _ CSRR
CSRI ð2Þ where CSRI and CSRR = CSR of initial and reinforced soil, respec- tively. The CSRI was calculated using the procedure of Seed and
Idriss (1971).
The shear stress reduction factor KG was calculated according to
Priebe (1998), Baez and Martin (1993), Goughnour and Pestana
(1998), and Rayamajhi et al. (2014), and mainly depends on the area replacement ratio η and the shear modulus ratio Gr.
(i.e., Gr is defined as the ratio of shear modulus of the stone column and that of the initial soil). It has been concluded that KG is inversely proportional to η and Gr. As the area replacement ratio increases, spacing between stone columns decreases. As such, stone columns provide more shear strength and decrease the shear stress in the reinforced soil. Moreover, after stone column reinforcement the shear modulus of reinforced soil is improved, and consequently the shear stress in the reinforced soil is decreased.
For a given value of CSR of initial soil, CSRI, the CSR of re- inforced soil, CSRR, was calculated by different approaches, as plotted in Fig. 2, adopting η _ 20% and Gr _ 5. The obtained
KG was 0.55, 0.58, 0.86, and 0.88, according to Baez and Martin
(1993), Priebe (1998), Goughnour and Pestana (1998), and
Rayamajhi et al. (2014) respectively. The greatest values of the
CSRR were predicted using the approach of Rayamajhi et al.
(2014), which accounts for the effects of flexure and shear strain incompatibility between the columns and the initial soil.
The lowest values were obtained using the approach of Baez and Martin (1993), based on the assumption of shear strain compatibility.

Conclusions
This paper assessed the effectiveness of stone columns in reducing liquefaction potential based on collected data derived from 24 case histories. The assessment focused on the mechanism of densifica- tion and stiffening. Their individual and combined effects were in- vestigated. The liquefaction potential was evaluated in terms of a safety factor. The evaluation of densification improvement was made using field tests, and the stiffening effects were analyzed through several approaches. Based on this liquefaction analysis, the following conclusions are drawn:
•
The installation of stone columns significantly increased soil density. Significant ground densification was achieved in clean to slightly silt sands, whereas densification of silt and clayey soils was very limited.
•
Varied assessments of the stiffening improvement of stone columns were developed. Comparative results showed that the assumption of shear strain compatibility gave the greatest shear stress reduction benefits. Approaches which account for the effects of bending and shear strain incompatibility between the columns and the initial soil provided less stiffening improve- ment. Further study investigating the factors affecting the amount of shear stress reduction may be beneficial. For future research, extensive experimental and numerical studies are required to assess the appropriate approach for evaluating the stiffening effect.
•
Densification and stiffening effects considerably improved the assessment of liquefaction potential of soil reinforced by stone columns. Liquefaction analysis that accounts for the combina- tion of densification and stiffening effects provides less potential risk of liquefaction than that obtained with individual effects.
Further study analyzing the stiffening effect by taking into account the real behavior of soil reinforced by stone columns under seismic loading appears to be warranted.
