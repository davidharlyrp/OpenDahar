Abstract: A
3
geogrid-encased stone column under an axial loading test has been numerically modeled with the discrete element method
7
(DEM) for a stone column wrapped by a finite difference method (FDM) shell-type encasement. The column was modeled using nonspherical
8
rigid particles considering the actual shape and size distribution of gravel, which better represents the discrete nature of stone columns. The
9
rationality of the proposed numerical model was successfully verified by experimental results from a previous model test. The complete
10
deformation and failure process of the geogrid-encased stone column has been well captured. The numerical results have been discussed at
11
both the macroscopic and microscopic levels. The stress state of the geogrid-encased stone column never approaches the limiting equilibrium
12
state before the geogrid rupture. The bearing capacity of the geogrid-encased stone column mainly depends on transverse ribs of the geogrid.
13
The force chain network and coordination number evolvements inside the stone column were determined during the complete loading process
14
and related to macroscopic failure behaviors. The parametric analysis indicates that linear relationships can be established between the
15
macroscopic performance of the geogrid-encased stone column and the properties of the geogrid and gravel. The macroscopic and micro-
16
scopic numerical results in this study can provide researchers improved understanding of the geogrid–gravel system. DOI: 10.1061/(ASCE)

19 4 Introduction
20
Stone
5
columns or granular piles, which are formed by compacted
21
gravels, are extensively used as ground reinforcements for various
22
foundations. A ground improvement technique using stone col-
23
umns has been successfully applied in recent decades, and is con-
24
sidered an economical and environmentally friendly method to
25
support structures and embankments (
6
Preibe 2005). However,
26
the stone column is cohesionless and cannot bear an external load
27
without a radial constraint provided by other materials (Hughes
28
et al. 1975). The application of stone columns may be limited when
29
there is a lack of sufficient radial constraint from the surrounding
30
soil. By encasing the stone column with cylindrical geogrids,
31
geogrid-encased stone columns have been successfully applied
32
in extremely soft soil in recent years (Yoo 2010; Almeida et al.
33
2015, 2018; Cengiz and Guler 2018; Zhou and Kong 2019).
34
The geogrid encasement greatly improves the stiffness and bearing
35
capacity of the stone columns and prevents the loss of gravels into
36
the soft soil. However, the reinforcement mechanism is not effec-
37
tively applied because it involves complex interactions between
38
granular materials and tensile resistant materials. Various studies
39
have been conducted to investigate the mechanical behaviors of
40
encased stone columns through experiments (Rajagopal et al. 1999;
41
7
Wu and Hong 2009; Chen et al. 2018; Xue et al. 2019), model tests
42
(Ghazavi and Afshar 2013; Hong et al. 2016; Miranda et al. 2017),
43
and numerical simulations (Murugesan and Rajagopal 2006; Yoo
44
2010; Mohapatra et al. 2017; Tan and Zhao 2018).
45
Most of the previous numerical studies were performed using
46
continuum-based models such as the finite element method (FEM)
47
and finite difference method (FDM). Recently, some researchers
48
introduced the discrete element method (DEM) to model stone col-
49
umns. These DEM models (Cundall and Strack 1979) have advan-
50
tages in simulating crushed rocks, gravels, or aggregates that are
51
beyond the capability of continuum models because of the discrete
52
nature of these discontinuous materials (Tan et al. 2015, 2016). A
53
series of DEM models has been used to simulate the stone column
54
by 2D disks and blocks to study the bearing mechanism of the
55
isolated stone column in soft soil (Indraratna et al. 2015; Tan et al.
56
2018, 2019, 2020b). Gu et al. (2017a, b) conducted 3D DEM sim-
57
ulations of a geogrid-encased stone column using graded particles
58
for gravels and parallel-bonded particles for the geogrid. The
59
simulation results from these studies were comparable to the exper-
60
imental data obtained from reported model tests. However, these
61
numerical studies lack accurate representative gravel shapes and
62
size distributions. The effects of the particle shape on the response
63
of geogrid–soil systems have been analyzed with respect to
64
geogrid-reinforced ballast (Stahl and Konietzky 2011; Stahl et al.
65
2014; Indraratna et al. 2014;
8
Ngo et al. 2014; Wang et al. 2016;
9
Gao
66
and Meguid 2018). These numerical studies show that the particle
67
shapes and size distribution strongly affect the deformation and
68
strength characteristics of granular materials. They have also been

Numerical Modeling of Geogrid-Encased Stone
83
Column
84
The numerical analysis conducted in the proposed study was
85
performed using the Particle Flow Code (PFC 6.0) and
10
Fast Lagran-
86

87
Modeling the Geogrid
88
The geogrid encasement, which has constrained interactions with
89
the stone column, can be simulated using shell-type geogrid ele-
90
ments in FLAC3D. The geogrid element behaves as an isotropic,
91
linear elastic material, the mechanical behaviors of which are
92
illustrated in
11
Fig. 1. The stresses that act on the geogrid structure,
93
consisting of the confining stress σm and total shear stress τ, are
94
balanced by the membrane stress N that develops within the
95
geogrid element itself. The confining stress σm is the normal stress
96
applied on the surface of the element, which can be induced by the
97
extrusion from the adjacent particles or grids. A tangential frictional
98
interaction occurs between the geogrid and the FLAC3D grid. The
99
elastic modulus, Poisson’s ratio, and thickness are used to represent
100
the elastic behavior of the geogrid element (Itasca 2017a). The
101
tensile limit or tensile strain limit has been added as an additional
102
parameter for geogrid elements through the FISH program in
103
FLAC3D. The element is deleted to simulate the rupture of the
104
geogrid when its maximum tensile stress (or strain) is higher than
105
the limit value.
106
The high-density polyethylene biaxial geogrid in the model test
107
conducted by Gu et al. (2017a) was simulated with a mesh of aper-
108
ture size 40 × 40 mm and a rib width of 5 mm. The stiffness and
109
tensile strength of the geogrid were obtained from a multirib tensile
110
test (
12
ASTM 2015). Thus, the parameters of the geogrid element can
111
be determined by simulating the direct tensile test on the geogrid
112
model, as shown in Fig. 2. The simulated stress–strain curve of the
113
geogrid model is compared with the experimental data in Fig. 2(a),
114
and the model parameters, shown in Table 1. The tensile strength
115
per unit width of the geogrid structure is 35 kN=m, and the tensile
116
limit of each geogrid rib is 1.5 kN (or 125 MPa in membrane
117
stress). Fig. 2(b) shows the tensile force distribution and final rup-
118
ture of the geogrid model in the direct tensile test.

Modeling Gravels
120
The shape and size distribution of the gravels are the key character-
121
istics used for simulating the discrete nature of granular materials in
122
the proposed numerical model. It is reasonable to assume that the
123
gravels are unbreakable, considering the stress state for stone col-
124
umns. A single gravel can be represented as a rigid body without a
125
failure limit. Therefore, the rigid block module in PFC 6.0 (Itasca
126
2017b) can be adopted to simulate the gravels, which introduces the
127
capability of modeling nonspherical rigid particles. The computa-
128
tional efficiency of the rigid block module is maximized because
129
the contact detection and resolution using the Gilbert–Johnson–
130
Keerthi algorithm (Gilbert et al. 1988) were adopted, which assumes
131
only one contact between two convex objects. The simulation pro-
132
cedure to model a gravel specimen under the large triaxial compres-
133
sion test is shown in Fig. 3.

First, several particle shapes were selected according to the most
135
common shapes extracted from actual gravels, simplified into 3D
136
convex polyhedra, and meshed in
13
CAD software. Then, the poly-
137
hedron models were input into the PFC as the shape templates. The
138
gravel specimen can be built by packing sufficient gravel particles
139
with the randomly selected shapes from the predetermined tem-
140
plates until the desired relative density is achieved. A large gravel
141
specimen with 300-mm diameter and 600-mm height was modeled
142
according to experimental data. The grain size distribution and rel-
143
ative density of the gravel specimen were measured after the
144
numerical model was generated to obtain true experimental data.
145
The grain size distribution in the numerical model was compared
146
with the experimental data from Gu et al. (2017a), as shown in
147
Fig. 4. The grain size distribution curve of the numerical model
148
was generally consistent with the experimental data, except in
149
the very small grain size range.
150
The contact behaviors between gravels are represented by the
151
linear contact bond model, which provides a linear spring that
152
acts in parallel with two gravels (Cundall and Strack 1979). The
153
linear spring of the contacts reveals the linear elastic (no-tension)
154
frictional behavior of gravels. Only three microscopic parameters
155
are needed for the contact model: contact Young’s modulus Ec,
156
normal-to-shear stiffness ratio kn=ks, and friction coefficient μ.
157
The method to determine the contact parameters was described
158
by Stahl and Konietzky (2011) and Tan et al. (2018). The contact
159
parameters can be roughly calculated following the determination
160
method provided by
14
Konietzky (2011). A large-scale triaxial test
161
simulation was conducted to more precisely determine the proper
162
contact parameters, given that the contact parameters also depend
163
on the gravel shapes, size distribution, and relative density. The
164
gravel specimens with a relative density of 0.72 were prepared
165
according to the method suggested by Salot et al. (2009) and Minh
166
and Cheng (2013). During the specimen preparation stage, the
167
densest specimen was prepared using a friction coefficient of
168
zero, since there was no frictional resistance to prevent the gravels
169
from moving. The looser specimens were prepared using higher
170
frictional coefficients. Several gravel specimens with different
171
frictional coefficients in the range of 0–5
15
were prepared by this
172
method. The void ratios of the specimens were measured after
173
the specimens reached static equilibrium under gravity with lateral
174
confinement; therefore, an empirical relationship between the void
175
ratio and the friction coefficient can be established for the proposed
176
gravel model. The minimum void ratio of 0.33 was observed in
177
the densest gravel model with zero friction coefficient, and the
178
maximum void ratio of ∼0.88 was observed in dense gravel models
179
with a friction coefficient higher than 3.0; these ratios are basically
180
consistent with statistical results of existing experiments (Wang and
181
Song 2019). The gravel specimen with the desired relative density
182
can be prepared by selecting a proper friction coefficient during the
183
specimen preparation stage.
184
The stress–strain curves of the simulated triaxial compression
185
test under confining pressures of 50, 100, and 200 kPa are illus-
186
trated in Fig. 5, which shows that the confining stress-dependent
187
stiffness, frictional strength, and dilatancy were well simulated.
188
The macroscopic Mohr–Coulomb parameters of the gravel speci-
189
men with a relative density of 0.72 are φ ¼ 41.8° and c ¼ 23 kPa.
190
The apparent cohesion of gravel specimens can be explained by
191
interlocking effects in gravel particles, which was also observed
192
in previous experimental studies (Gebrenegus et al. 2015; Chen
193
et al. 2018). The input microscopic parameters and observed mac-
194
roscopic properties are listed in Table 2. The mechanical response
195
of gravels can be properly captured by combining the gravel par-
196
ticle shape, size distribution, and corresponding contact parameters.
197
The observed macroproperties of the proposed gravel model are in
198
the typical range of parameters used for modeling stone columns
199
(Almeida et al. 2018). Gu et al. (2017a) adopted a high friction
200
coefficient of 0.8 and contact bond strength in their gravel model
201
to introduce the interlocking effect. These redundant parameters
202
were against common sense in granular physics, which is not nec-
203
essary if the gravel shape is being considered.

Modeling a Geogrid-Encased Stone Column
205
The axial loading test on an isolated geogrid-encased stone column
206
was conducted in the laboratory at Hunan University in China,
207
as shown in Fig. 6(a). The geogrid and gravel properties are as
208
described in previous sections. More details about the column
209
construction and loading procedure can be found in Gu et al.
210
(2017a). A geogrid-encased stone column with the identical dimen-
211
sions was modeled by the aforementioned gravel and geogrid
212
models, as shown in Fig. 6(b). PFC 6.0 shows the coupling scheme
213
by which force and displacement are transferred between geogrid
214
element nodes and particles through the PFC walls (Itasca 2017b).
215
The frictional resistance between geogrid ribs and gravels is con-
216
trolled by the friction coefficient between the wall and the gravel
217
particles. Some studies (Stahl et al. 2014; Gao and Meguid 2018)
218
indicated that the friction coefficient is in the range of 0.3–0.4. The
219
friction coefficient was set to 0.35 in the proposed model. Two rigid
220
walls were created on the top and bottom ends of the stone column
221
to represent the loading plate. The plate on the bottom was fixed
222
during displacement, and the axial stress was added under con-
223
trolled displacement by applying a downward velocity to the top
224
loading plate on the stone column. The loading velocity was mini-
225
mized to maintain a quasi-static equilibrium condition during the
226
loading process, which was 0.5 mm=min in the model test and
227
0.0004 mm=timestep in the numerical simulation.

Macroscopic Deformation and Failure Processes
253
To further investigate the deformation and failure characteristics
254
of the geogrid-encased stone column during the complete loading
255
process, several loading stages were selected and marked in the
256

257
of the proposed numerical model is plotted in Fig. 8, where stage
258
o is the initial state before loading, stages a–c are in the prefailure
259

260
The grayscale of the gravels represents the settlement magnitude
261
of each particle. The contour in the geogrid elements represents
262
the membrane stress (positive is tension).
263
The settlement of the gravels increases when the load increases,
264
and the magnitude of the settlement gradually decreases from top
265
to bottom because of the fixed bottom plate. Some gravels squeezed
266

267

268

269
stone column, which may be the reason for some fluctuation in
270
the prefailure range in the load-settlement curve. A small number
271

272

483
Conclusions
484
A geogrid-encased stone column has been numerically modeled
485
with a DEM stone column encased by FDM geogrid elements.
486
The shape and size distribution of the gravels that form the stone
487
column have been introduced to best represent the discrete nature
488
of the gravels in the DEM model. The geogrid-encased stone col-
489
umn under axial loading was simulated using the DEM-FDM
490
coupled numerical scheme, and the results were verified by exper-
491
imental data. Based on the numerical analysis, the main conclu-
492
sions are as follows:
493
1. The simulation results of the proposed numerical models and
494
experiments are consistent with each other. The complete
495
stress–strain behaviors of the geogrid-encased stone column
496
under axial loading were successfully predicted, including the
497
linear elastic relationship in the prefailure region and the strain
498
softening behavior in the postfailure region. The complete de-
499
formation and failure processes of the geogrid-encased stone
500
column have been well captured.
501
2. The proposed numerical scheme effectively captured the
502
mechanical interactions in the geogrid–gravels system. The ten-
503
sile force in the geogrid ribs is induced by the column expansion
504
and stabs from irregularly shaped gravels. The stress state of the
505
stone column never approaches the limiting equilibrium state,
506
even at the failure stage. The bearing capacity of the geogrid-
507
encased stone column mainly depends on the transverse rib of
508
the geogrid.
509
3. The DEM model provides the ability to visually investigate the
510
internal structural changes in the stone column from a micro-
511
scopic view. The force chain network and coordination numbers
512
inside the stone column were determined during the complete
513
loading process and were related to the macroscopic failure
514
mechanism.
515
4. The mechanical behaviors affected by the column and geogrid
516
properties were investigated through a parametric study. The
517
numerical results indicate that the properties of the geogrid
518
and gravels control the stiffness and bearing capacity of the en-
519
cased stone column. The linear relationship can be established
520
between the macroscopic performance of the geogrid-encased
521
stone column and properties of the geogrid and gravels.
