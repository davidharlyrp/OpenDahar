3D DEM-FDM Coupled Analysis of the Behavior of an Isolated Geogrid-Encased Stone Column under Axial Loading
Xin Tan1; Zhengbo Hu2; Changfu Chen3; and Minghua Zhao4
Abstract: A geogrid-encased stone column under an axial loading test has been numerically modeled with the discrete element method
(DEM) for a stone column wrapped by a finite difference method (FDM) shell-type encasement. The column was modeled using nonspherical rigid particles considering the actual shape and size distribution of gravel, which better represents the discrete nature of stone columns. The rationality of the proposed numerical model was successfully verified by experimental results from a previous model test. The complete deformation and failure process of the geogrid-encased stone column has been well captured. The numerical results have been discussed at both the macroscopic and microscopic levels. The stress state of the geogrid-encased stone column never approaches the limiting equilibrium state before the geogrid rupture. The bearing capacity of the geogrid-encased stone column mainly depends on transverse ribs of the geogrid.
The force chain network and coordination number evolvements inside the stone column were determined during the complete loading process and related to macroscopic failure behaviors. The parametric analysis indicates that linear relationships can be established between the macroscopic performance of the geogrid-encased stone column and the properties of the geogrid and gravel. The macroscopic and micro- scopic numerical results in this study can provide researchers improved understanding of the geogrid–gravel system. DOI: 10.1061/(ASCE)

Introduction
Stone columns or granular piles, which are formed by compacted gravels, are extensively used as ground reinforcements for various foundations. A ground improvement technique using stone columns has been successfully applied in recent decades, and is considered an economical and environmentally friendly method to support structures and embankments (Preibe 2005). However, the stone column is cohesionless and cannot bear an external load without a radial constraint provided by other materials (Hughes et al. 1975). The application of stone columns may be limited when there is a lack of sufficient radial constraint from the surrounding soil. By encasing the stone column with cylindrical geogrids, geogrid-encased stone columns have been successfully applied in extremely soft soil in recent years (Yoo 2010; Almeida et al.
2015, 2018; Cengiz and Guler 2018; Zhou and Kong 2019).
The geogrid encasement greatly improves the stiffness and bearing capacity of the stone columns and prevents the loss of gravels into the soft soil. However, the reinforcement mechanism is not effectively applied because it involves complex interactions between granular materials and tensile resistant materials. Various studies have been conducted to investigate the mechanical behaviors of encased stone columns through experiments (Rajagopal et al. 1999;
Wu and Hong 2009; Chen et al. 2018; Xue et al. 2019), model tests
(Ghazavi and Afshar 2013; Hong et al. 2016; Miranda et al. 2017), and numerical simulations (Murugesan and Rajagopal 2006; Yoo
2010; Mohapatra et al. 2017; Tan and Zhao 2018).
Most of the previous numerical studies were performed using continuum-based models such as the finite element method (FEM) and finite difference method (FDM). Recently, some researchers introduced the discrete element method (DEM) to model stone columns. These DEM models (Cundall and Strack 1979) have advantages in simulating crushed rocks, gravels, or aggregates that are beyond the capability of continuum models because of the discrete nature of these discontinuous materials (Tan et al. 2015, 2016). A series of DEM models has been used to simulate the stone column by 2D disks and blocks to study the bearing mechanism of the isolated stone column in soft soil (Indraratna et al. 2015; Tan et al.
2018, 2019, 2020b). Gu et al. (2017a, b) conducted 3D DEM simulations of a geogrid-encased stone column using graded particles for gravels and parallel-bonded particles for the geogrid. The simulation results from these studies were comparable to the experimental data obtained from reported model tests. However, these numerical studies lack accurate representative gravel shapes and size distributions. The effects of the particle shape on the response of geogrid–soil systems have been analyzed with respect to geogrid-reinforced ballast (Stahl and Konietzky 2011; Stahl et al.
2014; Indraratna et al. 2014; Ngo et al. 2014; Wang et al. 2016;
Gao and Meguid 2018). These numerical studies show that the particle shapes and size distribution strongly affect the deformation and strength characteristics of granular materials. They have also been

Numerical Modeling of Geogrid-Encased Stone
Column
The numerical analysis conducted in the proposed study was performed using the Particle Flow Code (PFC 6.0) and
Fast Lagran- gian Analysis in the Continua software program (FLAC3D 6.0).
Modeling the Geogrid
The geogrid encasement, which has constrained interactions with the stone column, can be simulated using shell-type geogrid ele- ments in FLAC3D. The geogrid element behaves as an isotropic, linear elastic material, the mechanical behaviors of which are illustrated in
Fig. 1. The stresses that act on the geogrid structure, consisting of the confining stress σm and total shear stress τ, are balanced by the membrane stress N that develops within the geogrid element itself. The confining stress σm is the normal stress applied on the surface of the element, which can be induced by the extrusion from the adjacent particles or grids. A tangential frictional interaction occurs between the geogrid and the FLAC3D grid. The elastic modulus, Poisson’s ratio, and thickness are used to represent the elastic behavior of the geogrid element (Itasca 2017a). The tensile limit or tensile strain limit has been added as an additional parameter for geogrid elements through the FISH program in
FLAC3D. The element is deleted to simulate the rupture of the geogrid when its maximum tensile stress (or strain) is higher than the limit value.
The high-density polyethylene biaxial geogrid in the model test conducted by Gu et al. (2017a) was simulated with a mesh of aper- ture size 40 × 40 mm and a rib width of 5 mm. The stiffness and tensile strength of the geogrid were obtained from a multirib tensile test (
ASTM 2015). Thus, the parameters of the geogrid element can be determined by simulating the direct tensile test on the geogrid model, as shown in Fig. 2. The simulated stress–strain curve of the geogrid model is compared with the experimental data in Fig. 2(a), and the model parameters, shown in Table 1. The tensile strength per unit width of the geogrid structure is 35 kN=m, and the tensile limit of each geogrid rib is 1.5 kN (or 125 MPa in membrane stress). Fig. 2(b) shows the tensile force distribution and final rup- ture of the geogrid model in the direct tensile test.
Modeling Gravels
The shape and size distribution of the gravels are the key character- istics used for simulating the discrete nature of granular materials in the proposed numerical model. It is reasonable to assume that the gravels are unbreakable, considering the stress state for stone col- umns. A single gravel can be represented as a rigid body without a failure limit. Therefore, the rigid block module in PFC 6.0 (Itasca
2017b) can be adopted to simulate the gravels, which introduces the capability of modeling nonspherical rigid particles. The computa- tional efficiency of the rigid block module is maximized because the contact detection and resolution using the Gilbert–Johnson–
Keerthi algorithm (Gilbert et al. 1988) were adopted, which assumes only one contact between two convex objects. The simulation pro- cedure to model a gravel specimen under the large triaxial compres- sion test is shown in Fig. 3.

First, several particle shapes were selected according to the most common shapes extracted from actual gravels, simplified into 3D convex polyhedra, and meshed in
CAD software. Then, the poly- hedron models were input into the PFC as the shape templates. The gravel specimen can be built by packing sufficient gravel particles with the randomly selected shapes from the predetermined tem- plates until the desired relative density is achieved. A large gravel specimen with 300-mm diameter and 600-mm height was modeled according to experimental data. The grain size distribution and rel- ative density of the gravel specimen were measured after the numerical model was generated to obtain true experimental data.
The grain size distribution in the numerical model was compared with the experimental data from Gu et al. (2017a), as shown in
Fig. 4. The grain size distribution curve of the numerical model was generally consistent with the experimental data, except in the very small grain size range.
The contact behaviors between gravels are represented by the linear contact bond model, which provides a linear spring that acts in parallel with two gravels (Cundall and Strack 1979). The linear spring of the contacts reveals the linear elastic (no-tension) frictional behavior of gravels. Only three microscopic parameters are needed for the contact model: contact Young’s modulus Ec, normal-to-shear stiffness ratio kn=ks, and friction coefficient μ.
The method to determine the contact parameters was described by Stahl and Konietzky (2011) and Tan et al. (2018). The contact parameters can be roughly calculated following the determination method provided by
Konietzky (2011). A large-scale triaxial test simulation was conducted to more precisely determine the proper contact parameters, given that the contact parameters also depend on the gravel shapes, size distribution, and relative density. The gravel specimens with a relative density of 0.72 were prepared according to the method suggested by Salot et al. (2009) and Minh and Cheng (2013). During the specimen preparation stage, the densest specimen was prepared using a friction coefficient of zero, since there was no frictional resistance to prevent the gravels from moving. The looser specimens were prepared using higher frictional coefficients. Several gravel specimens with different frictional coefficients in the range of 0–5 were prepared by this method. The void ratios of the specimens were measured after the specimens reached static equilibrium under gravity with lateral confinement; therefore, an empirical relationship between the void ratio and the friction coefficient can be established for the proposed gravel model. The minimum void ratio of 0.33 was observed in the densest gravel model with zero friction coefficient, and the maximum void ratio of ∼0.88 was observed in dense gravel models with a friction coefficient higher than 3.0; these ratios are basically consistent with statistical results of existing experiments (Wang and
Song 2019). The gravel specimen with the desired relative density can be prepared by selecting a proper friction coefficient during the specimen preparation stage.
The stress–strain curves of the simulated triaxial compression test under confining pressures of 50, 100, and 200 kPa are illus- trated in Fig. 5, which shows that the confining stress-dependent stiffness, frictional strength, and dilatancy were well simulated.
The macroscopic Mohr–Coulomb parameters of the gravel speci- men with a relative density of 0.72 are φ _ 41.8° and c _ 23 kPa.
The apparent cohesion of gravel specimens can be explained by interlocking effects in gravel particles, which was also observed in previous experimental studies (Gebrenegus et al. 2015; Chen et al. 2018). The input microscopic parameters and observed mac- roscopic properties are listed in Table 2. The mechanical response of gravels can be properly captured by combining the gravel par- ticle shape, size distribution, and corresponding contact parameters.
The observed macroproperties of the proposed gravel model are in the typical range of parameters used for modeling stone columns
(Almeida et al. 2018). Gu et al. (2017a) adopted a high friction coefficient of 0.8 and contact bond strength in their gravel model to introduce the interlocking effect. These redundant parameters were against common sense in granular physics, which is not nec- essary if the gravel shape is being considered.

Modeling a Geogrid-Encased Stone Column
The axial loading test on an isolated geogrid-encased stone column was conducted in the laboratory at Hunan University in China, as shown in Fig. 6(a). The geogrid and gravel properties are as described in previous sections. More details about the column construction and loading procedure can be found in Gu et al.
(2017a). A geogrid-encased stone column with the identical dimen- sions was modeled by the aforementioned gravel and geogrid models, as shown in Fig. 6(b). PFC 6.0 shows the coupling scheme by which force and displacement are transferred between geogrid element nodes and particles through the PFC walls (Itasca 2017b).
The frictional resistance between geogrid ribs and gravels is con- trolled by the friction coefficient between the wall and the gravel particles. Some studies (Stahl et al. 2014; Gao and Meguid 2018) indicated that the friction coefficient is in the range of 0.3–0.4. The friction coefficient was set to 0.35 in the proposed model. Two rigid walls were created on the top and bottom ends of the stone column to represent the loading plate. The plate on the bottom was fixed during displacement, and the axial stress was added under con- trolled displacement by applying a downward velocity to the top loading plate on the stone column. The loading velocity was mini- mized to maintain a quasi-static equilibrium condition during the loading process, which was 0.5 mm=min in the model test and
0.0004 mm=timestep in the numerical simulation.

Macroscopic Deformation and Failure Processes
To further investigate the deformation and failure characteristics of the geogrid-encased stone column during the complete loading process, several loading stages were selected and marked in the load-settlement curve in Fig. 7. The progressive failure process of the proposed numerical model is plotted in Fig. 8, where stage o is the initial state before loading, stages a–c are in the prefailure range, d is the peak strength stage, and e is in the postfailure range.
The grayscale of the gravels represents the settlement magnitude of each particle. The contour in the geogrid elements represents the membrane stress (positive is tension).
The settlement of the gravels increases when the load increases, and the magnitude of the settlement gradually decreases from top to bottom because of the fixed bottom plate. Some gravels squeezed out of the mesh apertures in the biaxial geogrid because of the dilatancy of the stone column under compression. These moving gravels induce a sudden change in the internal structure of the stone column, which may be the reason for some fluctuation in the prefailure range in the load-settlement curve. A small number of squeezed-out gravels does not lead to column failure because a strong radial constraint is provided by the geogrid encasement.

Microscopic Particle and Contact Behaviors
The DEM numerical model provides the ability to visually trace the gravel and internal structure changes of the stone column from a microscopic view. Fig. 13 illustrates the gravel displacement vector distribution during the complete loading process. Only the results showing a middle cut-plane of the stone column was plotted to avoid confusion caused by too many chaotic arrows. The drastic displacements of the gravels are generated in the upper half of the column. The compaction degree of the stone column increases during the loading process, as indicated by some voids being filled with moving gravels under loading.
Fig. 14(a) illustrates the force chain evolvement inside the stone column during the complete loading process. The force chain is the assembly of contact forces, which is graphically illustrated by cylindrical beams.
The diameter and color of the cylinders are scaled by the contact force magnitude. The force chains of the stone column before loading appear directionless and disorganized, and their contact forces are mostly below 0.3 kN. With an increase in settlement, the contact forces increase and reach the maximum value of 3.23 kN at peak load stage d. At postfailure stage e, the maximum contact force decreases to 3.1 kN. The force chain network does not immediately collapse after the peak load due to the constraint from the unbroken geogrid. The number of strong force chains (a contact force larger than 1.5 kN) is gradually in- creased with settlement, and the strong force chains tend to directly grow. As shown in the enlarged windows, a single strong force chain usually contains 3–4 gravels. Strong force chains connect with others and form the force chain network. The axial load is transmitted from top to bottom through the force chain network.
The anisotropy feature of the average contact forces during the loading process are best illustrated by rose diagrams, which are shown in Fig. 14(b), and were obtained by statistically determining the normal contact direction and distribution of the average contact forces at angular intervals of 10°.
Fig. 14. Force chain evolvement during the complete loading process: (a) force chains; and (b) orientation of contact normal forces.
Although there are considerably fewer strong force chains than weak chains, the strong force chains bear most of the external load.
Large movement of the gravels may destroy the stable structure of the force chain network and cause stress softening of the stone col- umn. The contact forces show an interesting trend in their predomi- nant directions according to their magnitudes. Fig. 15(a) shows the directions and quantity of the contact forces within specific ranges.
The strong contact force is minimal in quantity and shows a pre- dominant vertical direction. The number of weak contact forces is one order of magnitude higher than that of the strong forces and tends to increase. The anisotropy feature of the quantities of con- tacts under different levels of contact force is illustrated by rose diagrams in Fig. 15(b). The predominant direction of the weak con- tact forces indicates that they most likely induce radial stress in the stone column.
Another critical indicator related to the internal structure of the granular materials is the average coordination number, which is de- fined as the average number of contacts on one gravel in a certain region. The average coordination number commonly represents the packing stability and density of granular materials. The stone col- umn in a dense state has a high average coordination number. The average coordination number evolvements for different column parts during the complete loading process are shown in Fig. 16.
The initial average number varies from 6.3 to 6.6 depending on the column part. After being loaded, the average number increases and has a maximum value of 6.9 when the settlement reaches
11 mm. Then, the average number gradually decreases until column failure, reaching its minimum value of 6.0. According to the evolvement in the average coordination number as shown in Fig. 16, the mechanical behavior of the stone column can be divided into two distinctive stages: compaction and dilation. Continuous dila- tion causes tension in the geogrid encasement, which produces the radial constraint that acts on the stone column and maintains the stability of the stone column. The number in the middle column significantly decreases, which is consistent with the macroscopic bulging deformation as shown in Fig. 10(c).

Conclusions
A geogrid-encased stone column has been numerically modeled with a DEM stone column encased by FDM geogrid elements.
The shape and size distribution of the gravels that form the stone column have been introduced to best represent the discrete nature of the gravels in the DEM model. The geogrid-encased stone col- umn under axial loading was simulated using the DEM-FDM coupled numerical scheme, and the results were verified by exper- imental data. Based on the numerical analysis, the main conclu- sions are as follows:
1. The simulation results of the proposed numerical models and experiments are consistent with each other. The complete stress–strain behaviors of the geogrid-encased stone column under axial loading were successfully predicted, including the linear elastic relationship in the prefailure region and the strain softening behavior in the postfailure region. The complete de- formation and failure processes of the geogrid-encased stone column have been well captured.
2. The proposed numerical scheme effectively captured the mechanical interactions in the geogrid–gravels system. The ten- sile force in the geogrid ribs is induced by the column expansion and stabs from irregularly shaped gravels. The stress state of the stone column never approaches the limiting equilibrium state, even at the failure stage. The bearing capacity of the geogrid- encased stone column mainly depends on the transverse rib of the geogrid.
3. The DEM model provides the ability to visually investigate the internal structural changes in the stone column from a micro- scopic view. The force chain network and coordination numbers inside the stone column were determined during the complete loading process and were related to the macroscopic failure mechanism.
4. The mechanical behaviors affected by the column and geogrid properties were investigated through a parametric study. The numerical results indicate that the properties of the geogrid and gravels control the stiffness and bearing capacity of the en- cased stone column. The linear relationship can be established between the macroscopic performance of the geogrid-encased stone column and properties of the geogrid and gravels.
