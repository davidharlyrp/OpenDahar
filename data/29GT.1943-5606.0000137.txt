Mitigation of Liquefaction-Induced Lateral Deformation in a
Sloping Stratum: Three-dimensional Numerical Simulation
Ahmed Elgamal1; Jinchi Lu2; and Davide Forcellini3
Abstract: Finite-element FE simulations are increasingly providing a versatile environment for conducting lateral ground deformation
studies. In this environment, mitigation strategies may be assessed in order to achieve economical and effective solutions. On the basis of
a systematic parametric study, three-dimensional FE simulations are conducted to evaluate mitigation by the stone column SC and the
pile-pinning approaches. Mildly sloping saturated cohesionless strata are investigated under the action of an applied earthquake excitation.
For that purpose, the open-source computational platform OpenSees is employed, through a robust user interface that simpliﬁes the
effort-intensive pre- and postprocessing phases. The extent of deployed remediation and effect of the installed SC permeability are
investigated. The inﬂuence of mesh resolution is also addressed. Generally, SC remediation was found to be effective in reducing the sand
stratum lateral deformation. For a similar stratum with permeability in the silt range, SC remediation was highly ineffective. In contrast,
pile pinning appeared to be equally effective for the sand and silt strata permeability scenarios. Overall, the conducted study highlights the
potential of computations for providing insights toward the process of deﬁning a reliable remediation solution.

Introduction
Liquefaction-induced lateral spreading may result in signiﬁcant
damage, disruption of function, and considerable replacement ex-
pense for existing buildings and structures. When strong ground
shaking occurs, this type of adverse response is commonplace as
documented during the earthquakes of Niigata, Japan 1964,
Dagupan City, Philippines 1990, Chi-Chi, Taiwan 1999, and Ko-
caeli, Turkey 1999 Kishida 1966; Ohsaki 1966; Seed and Idriss
1967; Yoshimi and Tokimatsu 1977; Tokimatsu et al. 1991; Ada-
chi et al. 1992; Ishihara et al. 1993; Tokimatsu et al. 1994; Earth-
quake Engineering Research Institute EERI 2000, 2001
The risk of liquefaction and associated ground deformation
can be reduced by various ground improvement methods includ-
ing densiﬁcation, solidiﬁcation cementation, and gravel drains
or stone columns SCs Priebe 1991; Mitchell et al. 1995; Japa-
nese Geotechnical Society JGS 1998; Thevanayagam et al.
2001. Use of gravel drains/SCs is a rather recent development
compared with the more traditional soil densiﬁcation approaches
International Navigation Association INA 2001.
Gravel drains SCs as a liquefaction mitigation procedure was
initially studied by Seed and Booker 1977. Since then, the
gravel drain technique has attracted the attention of many re-
searchers e.g., Ishihara and Yamazaki 1980; Tokimatsu and
Yoshimi 1980; Baez and Martin 1995; Boulanger et al. 1998;
Brennan and Madabhushi 2002, practicing consultants e.g., Nip-
pon Kokan, Japan, http://www.nkk.co.jp and construction com-

Recently, considerable data e.g., Saxena and Hussin 1997;
Hayden and Baez 1994; Somasundaram et al. 1997; Soydemir et
al. 1997; Ashford et al. 2000; Adalier et al. 2003; Weaver et al.
2004 have shown that SC installation is an effective means of
ground improvement for mitigating liquefaction hazards. The SC
technique can combine the beneﬁcial effects of densiﬁcation, re-
inforcement, and increased drainage Shenthan et al. 2003.
Installation of piles to mitigate lateral spreading effects has
also been proposed Arulmoli et al. 2004; Boulanger and Toki-

pinning effect may provide an effective solution. As part of the
numerical investigation below, remediations by the SC technique

efﬁcacy.

Computational Formulation
All simulations were conducted using the open-source computa-
tional platform OpenSees http://opensees.berkeley.edu, Mazzoni
et al. 2006. This platform allows for developing applications to
simulate the performance of structural and geotechnical systems
subjected to static and seismic loadings.
Implemented in OpenSees Yang 2000; Yang and Elgamal
2002 is an analysis framework for saturated soil response as a
two-phase material following the u-p formulation of Chan 1988
and Zienkiewicz et al. 1990 where u is displacement of the soil
skeleton and p is pore pressure. This implementation is based on
the following assumptions: small deformation and rotation and
solid and ﬂuid density remain constant in both time and space;
porosity is locally homogeneous and constant with time; soil
grains are incompressible; and solid and ﬂuid phases are acceler-
ated equally.
In 3D, the soil domain is represented by 20-8 node, effective-
stress fully coupled solid-ﬂuid brick elements Lu 2006. As
such, 20 nodes describe the solid translational degrees of free-
dom, with the eight-corner nodes also describing the ﬂuid pres-
sure. The FE matrix equation is integrated in time using a single-
step predictor multicorrector scheme of the Newmark type Chan
1988; Parra 1996. For each time step, the solution is obtained
using the modiﬁed Newton-Raphson approach with Krylov sub-
space acceleration Carlson and Miller 1998; Mazzoni et al.
2006.
The soil constitutive model Figs. 1 and 2 implemented in
OpenSees Parra 1996; Yang and Elgamal 2002; Elgamal et al.
2003 was developed based on the multisurface-plasticity theory
for frictional cohesionless soils proposed by Prevost 1985. This

User-Interface for Nonlinear Ground-Foundation
Analysis
A graphical user-interface “OpenSeesPL” Fig. 3 was employed
to efﬁciently execute the parametric study Lu 2006. Via this
interface https://neesforge.nees.org/projects/openseespl, the FE
computations are conducted using the open-source platform Op-
enSees Mazzoni et al. 2006.
The capabilities of OpenSeesPL pre- and postprocessing fa-
cilitate a wide class of 3D studies. The point-and-click graphical
displays allow for: 1 deﬁnition of the pile geometry circular or
square pile and material properties; 2 deﬁnition of the 3D spa-
tial soil domain; 3 deﬁnition of the boundary conditions and
input seismic excitation or push-over analysis parameters; 4 se-
lection of soil materials from an available menu of cohesionless
and cohesive soil materials; and 5 convenient postprocessing
and graphical visualization of the analysis results including the
deformed mesh, ground response time histories, and pile response
Lu 2006. In the current version, OpenSeesPL may be employed
to study a number of geometries and conﬁgurations of interest
including:
1.
3D seismic ground response, where multiyield surface cohe-
sionless Drucker-Prager cone model and cohesive mises or
J2 soil models are available. The coupled solid-ﬂuid analysis
option allows for conducting liquefaction studies.
2.
Inclusion of a pile or shaft in the aforementioned 3D ground
mesh circular or square pile in a soil island. For the pile
response, linear, bilinear, and nonlinear ﬁber elements are
available in OpenSees Mazzoni et al. 2006. In addition to
the seismic excitation option, the pile system may be sub-
jected to monotonic or cyclic lateral push-over Lu 2006
loading in prescribed displacement or prescribed force
modes.
3.
Soil properties within the zone occupied by the pile as rep-
resented by pile diameter may be speciﬁed independently,
allowing for a variety of practical modeling scenarios. For
instance, various ground modiﬁcation approaches may be
studied by appropriate speciﬁcation of the material within the
pile zone. Among other options, liquefaction countermea-
sures in the form of gravel drains, SCs, and solidiﬁcation/
cementation may all be modeled and analyzed.
Of particular importance and signiﬁcance in the above scenarios
is the ability to simulate the presence of a mild inﬁnite-slope
conﬁguration, allowing for estimates of: 1 accumulated ground
deformation and efﬁcacy of a deployed liquefaction countermea-
sure and 2 liquefaction-induced lateral pile loads and resulting
moments/stresses.

Additional Important Practical Considerations
1.
The design considerations of a structure supported on or em-
bedded within the soil stratum ultimately dictate the tolerable
level of permanent lateral deformation i.e., the appropriate
Arr. High Arr values may be prohibitively expensive and
impractical to implement e.g., Arr exceeding 20%. The
same applies for the high SC permeability values in Tables 2
and 4.
2.
The above analyses do not address possible changes in soil
properties due to the installation process associated with dif-
ferent remediation approaches e.g., compaction of cohesion-
less soil around a driven pile. Such changes may become
more pronounced with the increase in Arr and can be ac-
counted for empirically by speciﬁcation of denser soil mod-
eling properties. On this basis, larger Arr values may actually
result in lower levels of accumulated lateral deformation
compared to the results reported in Tables 2–5. In practice
and design, this important aspect must receive due attention.
3.
Upon implementation of a stiffening ground improvement
approach as discussed above, a potential develops for
higher transmitted ground surface accelerations and/or shak-
ing intensities e.g., Figs. 9 and 14.
4.
The employed periodic-boundary modeling approach Fig. 4
applies to large remediated areas. Smaller remediated zones
with a relatively large perimeter to area ratio will be more
signiﬁcantly affected by the surrounding original stratum
characteristics. A more elaborate 3D model that includes
such a surrounding large unremediated soil domain may be
more appropriate in this case.
Summary and Conclusions
A study was conducted to explore the inﬂuence of SCs and pile
pinning on liquefaction-induced lateral deformation. The analyses
were conducted using OpenSees with the aid of OpenSeesPL, a
user interface that simpliﬁes the pre- and postprocessing phases.
In this study, an effort was made to explore the effectiveness
of the SC and the pile-pinning mitigation approaches by gradually
increasing the lateral extent of remediation, in order to achieve a
satisfactory lower level of permanent deformation. For this repre-
sentative study, a 10-m layer of liqueﬁable sand or nonplastic silt
was subjected to the 1987 Wildlife refuge downhole NS ground
motion.
Generally, SC remediation was found to be effective in reduc-
ing the sand stratum lateral deformation. The SC permeability
was found to play a signiﬁcant role in this case, with virtually no
permanent deformation as the SC k and Arr approached 0.01 m
and 20%, respectively. However, for a similar stratum with per-
meability in the silt range, SC remediation was highly ineffective,
regardless of the employed SC permeability.
In contrast, pile pinning appeared to be equally effective, both
for the sand and the silt strata. A replacement ratio as low as 10%
was found to possibly provide satisfactory outcomes.
