2. Determination of soil strength parameters for the analysis of highway slope failures
G. I. CRABB, BSc, MICE, Transport and Road Research Laboratory, and J. H. ATKINSON, PhD, FICE,
FGS, Professor of Soil Mechanics, City University, UK
Laboratory tests have been carried out on many of the plastic clays from S.E. England which have given rise to highway earthwork slope failures. These included routine and special low-stress triaxial tests on tubed and reconstituted samples; in the special tests the samples were brought to failure along stress paths similar to those expected in the field. The tests gave values for the peak and critical strengths of the soils. The results indicated that the stability of the shallow slips was controlled by the critical or fully softened friction angle.
INTRODUCTION
1. Shallow failures of embankment and cutting slopes have been common on motorways and trunk roads constructed in the plastic clays in S.E. England over the past 25 years.
The extent of these failures has been surveyed by the Transport and Road Research Laboratory
(ref. 1) and three slopes at risk of failure have been closely monitored over a period of several years, during which time two have failed (ref. 2).
2. A characteristic feature of these failures was that they were all occurring on slip planes parallel to the slope and at depths of lm to 2m where the stresses in situ were of the order of 15 kPa to 30 kPa. These stresses are very much smaller than those used in conventional laboratory tests and are in the range where the peak failure envelope is curved
(ref. 3). Further, since the slips involved relatively large movements over several years there is the possibility that the soils had softened such that their strengths had reduced to the fully softened, or critical, state (ref.
4).

SOIL PARAMETERS APPROPRIATE TO ANALYSIS OF
SHALLOW SLIPS
7. The ultimate stability of a slope occurs in the long term when pore pressures have reached their steady state values. These will be such that the water contents are in equilibrium and inflow of rain and groundwater is balanced by outflow by drainage and through vegetation. In the present cases field observations indicate that equilibrium pore pressures may be close to zero at depths of lm to 2m below the slope surface. Since some drainage and pore pressure changes have occurred since construction, and, since pore pressures are known (or assumed) it is appropriate to carry out stability analyses in terms of effective, not total, stresses.
8. Fig 4 (a) illustrates a typical stress- strain curve for soil. There is a peak at P at relatively small strain and there is a critical strength C after larger strains. The critical strength shown in Fig 4(a) is often called the ultimate strength (ref. 5) or the fully softened strength (ref. 4 ) . After very large movements on slip surface there may be a lower residual strength (ref. 6); this is quite different from the critical strength which occurs typically after strains in the range 10% to 30% and is characterised by continued straining at constant volume and constant effective stress. The residual strength is associated with highly polished slip surfaces in which clay particles have become aligned in directions parallel with the direction of sliding and is relevant only after displacements of the order of several metres.

MEASUREMENT OF SOIL PARAMETERS IN LABORATORY
TESTS
13. The most convenient test to measure soil strength is the triaxial test described in detail by Bishop and Henkel (ref. 8). Direct and simple shear tests are also used but the results can be difficult to interpret (ref. 9)
Triaxial tests may be drained or undrained and they are usually carried out with constant cell pressure. During a conventional drained triaxial test the shear and the normal effective stresses both increase and so the stress paths correspond to OP or NC in Fig 5.
Also shown in Fig 5 are special stress paths AS which may be achieved by increasing the pore pressure while the total stresses are held constant.
14. Three failure envelopes are illustrated in Fig 5; these are the critical failure envelope CC, the linear Mohr-Coulomb peak envelope PP and the curved peak envelope SS appropriate at small effective stresses.
15. Conventional triaxial compression tests on tubed or reconstituted overconsolidated samples (paths OP) are usually carried out at normal stresses in the range 50 kPa to 300 kPa.
These tests conveniently define an approximately linear part of the peak envelope.
They cannot, however, examine the curved peak envelope at small effective stress. Tests on overconsolidated samples are unsuitable for measuring the critical strength of soils as slip planes usually develop in the samples beyond the peak so that accurate measurements of stress, pore pressure and strain become difficult (ref. 10).
16. Conventional drained or undrained triaxial compression tests on normally consolidated samples (paths NC) are required to determine the critical failure envelope with precision.
For triaxial samples these may require reconsolidation to relatively high effective stresses or, alternatively, tests may be carried out on reconstituted samples at modest stress levels.

The intact soil was obtained by high quality thin-wall tube sampling.
The reconstituted material was obtained by oven drying, pulverising, mixing with water to a slurry and reconsolidation.

Field observations of slopes in Gault Clay show that pore pressure measured in situ at depths corresponding to the observed slip planes was generally close to zero. These results suggests that the critical failure envelope, rather than the peak failure envelope, controls slope stability with shallow planar slip surfaces.
CONCLUSIONS
25.
Failures of cut and fill slopes in motorway earthworks slopes in plastic clays in
S.E. England occurred typically by slipping along planes parallel to the slope surface and at depths of 1 to 2 m where the porewater pressures were typically close to zero.

26. Conventional drained or undrained triaxial or shear tests carried out at normal stresses in the range 50kPa to 300kPa are generally unable to measure the soil strength parameters appropriate to these failures.
27. Simple analyses of slope failures indicated that their stability could be assessed in terms of their critical (or fully softened) strength. There was no evidence that the stability of the slopes was governed by the peak strength, even allowing for the curvature of the peak failure envelope at low effective stress.
