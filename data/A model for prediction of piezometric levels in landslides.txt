7. A model for prediction of piezometric levels in landslides
R. FELL, BE(Civil), MEngSc, Professor of Civil Engineering, T. G. CHAPMAN, BSc, PhD, Emeritus
Professor and P. K. MAGUIRE, BSc, GradDip, AACS, University of New South Wales, Australia
A hydrologic process model has been developed to predict piezometric levels in landslides. The model uses daily rainfall
and pan evaporation records, and takes account of runoff, infiltration, variations in soil moisture content in the unsaturated
zone, evapotranspiration, and the response of the piezometric level to the resultant recharge to the groundwater. The model
parameters are physically identifiable variables relating to the infiltration, evapotranspiration and recharge processes.
Calibration against 3-1/2 years of records showed that piezometric levels can be modelled with reasonable accuracy granted
the heterogeneous nature of landslide debris, and the limitations of using daily rainfall records. The model can be used to
assess the probability of future landsliding by statistical analysis of piezometric levels predicted from historic rainfall and
evaporation records. This can be useful in decision making relating to the economics of stabilization works.
INTRODUCTION
1. The probability that a landslide will become active is
commonly dependent on the piezometric levels in the
landslide, which in turn depend mainly on rainfall. Many
attempts to model the fluctuation of piezometric levels with
rainfall are based on an empirical approach, with little
attempt to model the physical processes involved in
infiltration, runoff and groundwater response (refs. 1-8).
2. Other models eg. (refs. 9 and 10) are based on the
overall water balance with ref. 10 making some attempt to
model the physical processes on a monthly time scale.
3. Yet another group of models use the one dimensional
flow equation in the unsaturated zone eg. (refs. 11-14).
These require pluviograph data, rather than the daily rainfall
data which are more commonly available, and require a
knowledge of soil parameters such as hydraulic
conductivity which are difficult to determine with any
degree of accuracy, particularly in such heterogeneous
materials as landslides.
4. The model described in this paper was developed to
make use of daily rainfall data, pan evaporation, and
piezometric data available for a landslide at Speers Point in
Newcastle, Australia. The aim was to develop a model
with a simple structure but which used parameters which
related to the physical processes involved. Piezometric
records were available for 3-1/2 years, culminating in
reactivation of landsliding for the first time in 35 years.

8. The equations can be applied to a single location in the
landslide and calibrated against a ppiezometer at that
location. Alternatively the piezometric levels in a series of
piezometers down the slope can be modelled. In this case
Ko for the uphill location becomes R{ for the next location
after adjustment for the relative areas represented by the
piezometers. This can allow modelling of a catchment area
upslope of the landslide.
9. 
The model is sensitive to variations in the
parameters, and it is necessary to calibrate against actual
rainfall, pan evaporation and piezometer readings.
However it is useful to appreciate the likely order of
magnitude of the parameters and the following is given for
guidance:
(a) 
Field Capacity in Root Zone (SCAP)
Values may range from 0.05D for coarse sandy soils to
0.4D for clay soils, where D is the rooting depth of the
main vegetation species.
(b) 
Storage Capacity Moisture Content (SDRY)
Values may range from 0.02D for coarse sandy soils to
0.2D for clay soils.
(c) 
Recharge Coefficient (A^
This parameter has a very wide range. For daily models,
values have been reported from less than 0.01 for clay soils
to over 0.9 for freely draining sandy soils.
(d) 
Evapotranspiration Coefficients A2 and A3
A2, the crop coefficient, is the ratio of transpiration of
unstressed vegetation to pan evaporation. It depends on the
type of vegetation, stage of growth and season. Values are
generally in the range 0.6 to 0.8, with the lower values in
the winter months. However lower and higher values have
been reported for eucalypt forest (eg. Sharma, 1984)).
A3 is the maximum rate at which a particular vegetation-soil
complex can transpire. 
It depends on climate, soil
characteristics, and type and stages of growth of vegetation.
Values for native vegetation in Australia have been in the
range 5-25mm/day. Refs. 15-17 have values of A2 and A3
for a range of vegetation.
THE SPEERS POINT LANDSLIDES
10. Figure 2 shows a section through the Chelston St
landslide at Speers Point. The landslide is about 200m x
200m in plan and up to 10m deep. There is another
landslide lkm to the south — the Thompson Rd landslide
which has similar characteristics. It is 400m x 200m in
plan and up to 20m deep.
11. The landslides occur within the Newcastle Coal
Measures, and are underlain by claystone from the Awaba
Tuff and coal from the Fassiefern Seam. The bedding is
horizontal to 2° dip out of the slope. Sliding has occurred
on planes parallel to the bedding, but at several levels as
shown in Figure 2. The landslide colluvium is mainly
sandy clay and clayey sand derived from conglomerate but
also contains boulders of conglomerate in the upslope part
of the slide mass.
12. Well developed slide plane(s) have been located in
soils largely derived from the claystone at the base of
sliding. Direct shear testing indicates a strength of c'=0,
<])'=120 is applicable, and this is backed up by back-analysis
of sliding which occurred in 1988. Vegetation in the area is
mostly paspalum grass used for horse grazing.

APPLICATION OF THE MODEL TO THE SPEERS
POINT LANDSLIDES
14. During 1984 and 1985 Casagrande type piezometers
were installed to measure groundwater pressures in the
Chelston St and Thompson Rd landslides. The piezometer
tips were installed at around the base of the landslide
colluvium and were sealed by backfilling the borehole with
bentonite. 
Seventeen piezometers were installed at
Chelston St. The piezometers were monitored over the
period November 1984 to April 1988. Readings were
generally taken weekly, with daily readings when
piezometric levels were high, and some gaps with
underspaced readings.
15. Daily rainfall data for this period were available for
a rainfall station 3km away. Class A evaporation data were
available from 30km away. As the intention was to model
over the period of rainfall record (1900-1988), and
evaporation records were only available after 1974, mean
daily pan evaporation for each month was used.
16. The model was applied to the recorded data at
Chelston St. The steps involved were:
(a) 
select piezometers representative of the landslide.
This was done by consideration of response times and
levels, and location within the landslide. Piezometers
used were SB 107, PC12, PC6 and PCI 1.
(b) 
After further consideration of response times (which
were generally less than 3 days, but up to 11 days)
and taking account of the variations of response of
adjacent piezometers, results from pairs of adjacent
(10 to 20m apart) piezometers were also modelled.
Hence results from PC12 and 13; PC6 and 8; and
PC7 and 11 were averaged.
(c) 
Recession curves for the main district rainfall events in
November 1984, April 1985 and July 1986 were
plotted and the A4 coefficient estimated. This showed
that the results plotted quite well to satisfy the log-
linear relationship assumed in equation [7], but that
A4 was not the same for each rainfall event, and the
plot was often bi-linear, with a different (usually
smaller) A4 value applying for the lower part of the
recession curve. Figure 3 shows some of the plots.
This bi-linear behaviour was built into the model
where appropriate.
(d) 
Using this information, and an estimated range of
values for the other coefficients, the model was
calibrated by using a modification of the simplex
minimisation algorithm developed by ref. 18 to
determine the optimum values of the parameters listed
in Table 1. This was done by minimising the root
mean square of the differences between observed and
predicted piezometric heads.

DISCUSSION OF THE RESULTS
19. It can be seen that the model produces a moderately
successful simulation over the period of record. There are
however significant errors with some rainfall events not
producing the recorded piezometric response in some
piezometers. It was found that similar RMS errors could be
achieved with quite different parameters, and these
sometimes gave better simulation of individual events not
well modelled by the optimised simulation. For example,
the large differences in SCAP and SDRY are a result of
optimisation giving two areas of minimum RMS ie. with a
large and small difference in SCAP and SDRY,
compensated by differences in the other parameters.
20. It can be seen that the individual piezometers give a
better result than modelling the paired piezometers. This
can be related to the averaging of piezometer responses,
which in practice showed different response times for

27. It can be seen that there is some correlation between
these maximum levels and recorded landsliding at Chelston
St and Thompson Rd landslides, giving some confidence
that the model is relevant to landsliding at this location. The
1988 landsliding coincided with piezometric levels reaching
near ground surface levels over the whole of the landslide.
Somewhat better ranking can be achieved by using a larger
RMS error but modelling the peak levels of the 1985 event
more closely.
28. By ranking the maximum predicted piezometric
levels in any piezometer (or pair of piezometers), the
probability of a certain piezometric level being exceeded can
be determined. If for example, stability analysis shows that
the factor of safety will be 1.0 when piezometric levels
reach ground surface (as was the case for the Chelston St
landslide) the probability of landsliding can be determined
by the probability that piezometric levels reach ground
surface.
29. Where it is apparent that piezometric levels need to
reach ground surface over the whole of the landslide then
the probability of each piezometer reaching ground surface
must be considered. When this was done for the Chelston
St landslide the probability of landsliding was ~l in 10 to
25 years, depending on the model being used.
CONCLUSIONS
30. When the model was first being developed it was a
prerequisite that the parameters used could be related to
physical properties of the landslide material and climate. In
the outcome it has been shown that the model is very
sensitive to the assumptions made, and needs calibration
with a lengthy period of piezometric readings, effectively
making it a semi empirical approach. While this is clearly a
shortcoming it is considered likely to be common to all
modelling techniques, since the alternatives require
knowledge of soil parameters such as hydraulic

conductivity, porosity, capillary pressure heads etc which
are themselves difficult to measure and invariably have a
wide scatter of values in landslide colluvium.
31.The reality is also that in many landslides, only
limited data will be available, including daily rainfall, and in
those cases a semi empirical approach such as this model is
all that is practicable.
32. The model will not have universal applicability,
being inadequate in steep slopes with shallow soil cover
with rapid response times. Being daily rainfall based, it will
only be applicable when relatively lengthy antecedent rainfall
leads to the initial condition — eg. the 20 to 60 days which
are critical at Speers Point. It will also only apply where
infiltration is essentially vertical without significant recharge
from under the landslide.
