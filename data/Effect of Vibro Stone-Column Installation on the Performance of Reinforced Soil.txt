Effect of Vibro Stone-Column Installation on the Performance of Reinforced Soil
Kareem Al Ammari, Ph.D.1; and Barry G Clarke, Ph.D., C.Eng.2
Abstract: Empirical design methods for stone-column foundations are often on single stone columns or as a homogeneous medium of soil and column. These methods underestimate the capacity of the composite system because they do not take into account the increased confining stress acting on the stone column or the increased stiffness of the soil. This study used PLAXIS 2D to study the effect of the installation method on the confining pressure and soil stiffness around a single column by assuming the installation of the column could be modeled as an expanding cavity followed by consolidation of the surrounding soil. The mean stress and stiffness generated during installation between two adjacent columns was used in PLAXIS 3D to compare the settlement of circular foundations on estuarine deposits reinforced by stone columns at a site in Santa Barbara, California. Good agreement was found between the predicted and actual settlement of the trial foundations on three column arrangements. The predictions gave a better estimate of the settlement compared with those using a unit cell or homogeneous medium, showing that improvements to the soil should be taken into account when assessing stone-column performance. Introduction
The majority of methods to design stone columns, according to
Sexton and McCabe (2012), are based on a unit cell (e.g., Aboshi et al. 1979; Balaam and Booker 1985; Hu 1995; Priebe 1995;
McKelvey et al. 2004) in which a settlement improvement factor is related to the area replacement ratio of the cell. This was shown by McCabe et al. (2009), using case studies, to be an acceptable method. However, a method to install a stone column, the bottom- feed method, expands the stone column, increasing the confining stress on the stone column, and, because of the increase in stress in the surrounding soil, increases the stiffness of the soil. Kirsch
(2006) showed that, in sandy soils, the increase in stiffness was permanent to a distance of four to eight times the column diameter, which exceeds typical column arrangements; that is, the stiffness of the composite system of soil and stone columns exceeds that of the unit cell. Al Ammari and Clarke (2016) showed that the settlement of a rigid foundation on a unit cell (single column) was reduced by
55% if the increases in soil stiffness and confining stress due to the expanding stone column (Hughes and Withers 1974) were taken into account. The increase in stiffness is due to the dissipation of excess pore pressures generated during installation (e.g., Castro and
Sagaseta 2009; Debats et al. 2003; Gab et al. 2007; Guetif et al.
2007; Kirsch 2006). Killeen and McCabe (2014) suggested that the increase in stiffness is offset by the remolding of the soil around the column, though Kirsch (2006) suggested that the increase in stiffness would be permanent.
Therefore, rather than model a stone column as an independent unit, it would be better to model the stone columns and surrounding soil as a system to take into account the increase in confining stress on the columns and the increase in stiffness of the soil. Sexton and
McCabe (2015) suggested that most numerical studies of stone- column capacity were based on full replacement columns with an arbitrarily assigned value of confining stress. Kirsch (2006) and
Sexton and McCabe (2015) modeled displacement columns by starting with a full replacement column to represent the probe to determine the increase in confining stress in a unit cell analysis using cavity expansion.
In this study, the installation of the stone columns started with a full replacement column to model the probe. This was converted to a displacement column to model the installation of the stone col- umn using cavity expansion to estimate the improvement in the stiffness of the soil, which, together with the stone column, created a composite system. The increase in stiffness of the surrounding soil due to the installation of a stone column was modeled using
PLAXIS 2D and the stiffness of the composite system was modeled using PLAXIS 3D.
Field Case: Santa Barbara Waste Treatment Plant
The field case selected to validate the results of the numerical model was a wastewater treatment plant in Santa Barbara, Califor- nia (Fig. 1), which had been studied by Mitchell and Huber (1985),
Elshazly et al. (2006, 2007, 2008), and Killeen (2012). The Santa
Barbara wastewater treatment site is about 2.5 m above sea level.
The site stratigraphy (Fig. 2) is formed of
•
1–3 m of recent fill formed of clayey sand containing a mixture of anthropogenic wastes including asphalt, masonry, wood, glass, and metals.
•
5–16 m of estuarine deposits that increase in thickness from northeast to southwest across the site. They consist of layers of silty and sandy to clayey and silty sand, with some local lenses of sand or gravel.

Stone columns were chosen by the design engineers because the site preparation time was limited to 6 months to avoid any damages that might occur to the adjacent light industrial structures if the ground water table was lowered to form conventional foundations and to prevent liquefaction in this seismically active area (Mitchell and Huber 1985). More than 6,500 stone columns were constructed using the top feed installation method in which a current of water is jetted from the nose of the vibrator to aid the penetration into the soft soil, creating 0.50- to 0.75-m-diameter boreholes.
When the vibrator reached the desired depth, well-graded gravel
(12–100 mm) was introduced into the annular space between the probe and the borehole wall and was pushed down the borehole by the action of the probe. The probe was moved up and down in the borehole to compact the gravel, thus expanding the walls of the borehole. The final diameter of the stone columns varied between
0.81 and 1.22 m (Mitchell and Huber 1985). Three different stone column arrangements were used: 1.2 × 1.5 m for a bearing pres- sure of 145 kPa, limiting the settlement to 6 mm; 1.75 × 1.75 m for medium loads; and 2.10 × 2.10 m for a bearing pressure of
60 kPa. The stone columns were founded in the older marine de- posits resulting in 9–15 m long stone columns in the overlying estuarine deposits (Mitchell and Huber 1985).
Twenty-eight field loading tests were performed with 1-, 2-, and
2.2-m-diameter, 1.2-m-deep concrete footings centered on the col- umns that corresponded to the three cases of stone column arrange- ments. Modeling Framework
Stone columns have been analyzed as either
•
A uniform layer of a soil with a stiffness equivalent to the volumetric stiffness of the composite system (homogenized model); or
•
As axisymmetric studies of a single column surrounded by soil with adjacent columns modeled as a thin cylinder of stone
(unit cell model).
Neither of these methods take into account the increase in stiffness of the surrounding soil, nor do they model the arrangement of the columns. A three-dimensional (3D) analysis, in this case
PLAXIS 3D, can model the stone columns, but is unable to model the undrained cavity expansion and large strains associated with that expansion (McCabe et al. 2009). Therefore, PLAXIS 2D was used as a first step to estimate the improvement in both the coef- ficient of lateral earth pressure and stiffness of the different estua- rine soil layers using an axisymmetric model of a single column.
The process was as follows:
1. The installation of a single stone column was asymmetrically modeled in PLAXIS 2D as a displacement column using the principle of cavity expansion (Clarke 1994) similar to Stage 1 of the numerical modeling used by Sexton and McCabe (2015).
This produces a variation in stress and stiffness in the surround- ing soil for a single column.
2. The installation of the adjacent columns also increases the stress and stiffness of the surrounding soil. This was modeled in
PLAXIS 2D by installing a second column in a soil with the mean stress and stiffness produced from the first stage.
3. The mean stress and stiffness from the second stage was used in
PLAXIS 3D to model the stone column arrangements at Santa
Barbara, California, comparing the predicted settlements with those observed in the field tests.
The hardening soil model was considered the most appropriate model for simulating the relevant features of the fine- and coarse- grained soils because it takes into account the stress dependency of stiffness moduli and accounts for shear and volumetric hardening.

Conclusions
Empirical design of stone column installations is based on the concept of compacted columns restrained by the lateral pressure in the surrounding soil. Stone columns also improve the stiffness of the soil creating a composite foundation, which is stiffer than the stone columns on their own. It is possible to model this composite foundation by producing a homogeneous system with a stiffness equal to the combined stiffness of the soil and columns based on their volumes. This paper developed that concept by analyzing the composite system in which the installation of the stone columns increases the lateral stress and stiffness of the surrounding soil and the increased stiffness of the surrounding soil was used in the numerical analysis. This approach was compared with those based on the single column and homogenized methods and validated against a case study by comparing the predicted settlement with that observed in field tests using a circular foundation. It is con- cluded that
•
This approach to a composite foundation predicted less settle- ment than those based on the single column and homogenized methods;
•
This approach predicted similar settlements to those of the trial foundations on stone columns installed at different centers in estuarine deposits; and
•
The design of vibro stone column foundations should take into account the increase in lateral stress within the surrounding soil and the increased stiffness of those soils.
