Modeling the Performance of Stone Column–Reinforced
Soft Ground under Static and Cyclic Loads
Sudip Basack, Ph.D., M.ASCE1; Buddhima Indraratna, Ph.D., F.ASCE2;
and Cholachat Rujikiatkamjorn, Ph.D., M.ASCE3
Abstract: Installing stone columns is a convenient method of soft ground improvement. Although several analytical and numerical solutions
are available to predict the performance of stone column–improved soft ground, these models are incapable of capturing the influence of
cyclic loading on transport corridors, such as highways and railways. The authors developed a novel finite-difference model adopting
modified Cam clay theory to analyze the response of stone column–reinforced soft soil under static and cyclic loadings. Apart from predicting
excess pore water pressure dissipation and the resulting settlement, the model also captures the effect of cyclic loading by considering
yield surface contraction. The solutions developed were validated using available field observations and laboratory test results. The
model was successfully applied to a selected case study at the Australian National Field Testing Facility at Ballina, New South Wales,
Australia. The limitations of the proposed model are highlighted as well. DOI: 10.1061/(ASCE)GT.1943-5606.0001378. © 2015 American
Society of Civil Engineers.
Author keywords: Finite-difference method; Foundation settlement; Constitutive models; Cyclic loads; Soft soils; Stone columns.
Introduction
Reducing the long-term settlement of infrastructure and providing
cost-effective foundations with sufficient load-bearing capacities
are essential requirements for infrastructure development in most
countries (Black et al. 2007). In particular, soft soil foundations
can cause excessive settlement, initiating undrained failure in the
absence of effective ground improvement (Indraratna et al. 2013).
Over several decades, different ground improvement techniques
have been developed, including stone columns, preloading with
vertical
drains,
piling,
geogrids,
and
chemical
stabilization
(Sivakumar et al. 2004).
Reinforcing soft ground by installing stone columns is a well-
established technique practiced worldwide (Wang 2009). The
concept involves partial replacement of weak soil with compacted
vertical columns of stones that act as in situ reinforcement to the
soft cohesive soil. Stone columns can be used to support embank-
ments, bridge abutments, and tanks, as well as industrial, commer-
cial, and marine structures (Townsend and Anderson 2004).
The advantages of this method include increased bearing capacity,
reduced postconstruction settlement and lateral movement of soil,
improved slope stability, and liquefaction control (Guetif et al.
2007; Bouassida and Hazzar 2012; Bouassida and Carter 2014).
In the field, the stone columns are usually installed in a triangu-
lar, square, or hexagonal pattern (Wang 2009). The equivalent
diameter, de, of the influence zone and the column spacing, s,
can be correlated by introducing a geometrical constant cg as
(Balaam and Booker 1981)

Owing to the flexibility of transport embankments, the adoption
of a free-strain hypothesis is likely to better match the field condi-
tions than an equal-strain approach, although an intermediate state
between the free strain and equal strain conditions is observed in the
field (Indraratna et al. 2013). Numerous analytical and numerical
studies have been conducted to study stone column–reinforced soft
ground performance under both free-strain (Alamgir et al. 1996;
Indraratna et al. 2013) and equal-strain (Wang 2009; Han and Ye
2001, 2002; Murugesan and Rajagopal 2010) conditions. Under
free-strain conditions, the applied surcharge induces an equal distri-
bution of surface load, resulting in uneven surface settlement
(Barron 1947). The flexibility of the embankment essentially de-
pends on its material density and thickness (Terzaghi 1943).
While the unit cell analogy appears to successfully capture the
behavior of soil improved by stone columns near the embankment
centerline (Lorenzo and Bergado 2004; Murugesan and Rajagopal
2010), this approach may not predict the true behavior at a signifi-
cant distance from the centerline, for several reasons, including
large strain conditions, column distortion, plastic deformation,
and heaving at toe. Another shortcoming in the majority of existing
solutions is the simplified assumption of a linear void ratio–
effective stress correlation of soft soil, which necessitates the ap-
propriate choice of the value of soil compressibility. While
recent solutions are available for void ratio–dependent soil com-
pressibility and radial consolidation facilitated by vertical drains

For a typical fill embankment, the high column-to-soil stiffness
ratio initiates an arching effect of the surcharge fill over the stone
columns (Deb 2010), resulting in an uneven distribution of vertical
stress on the ground surface, even under free strain (Indraratna et al.
2013). The installation of stone columns introduces significant dis-
turbance in the soft soil surrounding the columns, referred to by
many researchers as the smear zone (Han and Ye 2002; Walker
and Indraratna 2006; Wang 2009). Centrifuge model tests con-
firmed the existence of a zone of disturbance around the columns
in several distinct sections (Weber et al. 2010). During consolida-
tion, an excessive hydraulic gradient at the soil–column interface
sets off the migration of clay particles to the column pores, initiat-
ing clogging (Adalier and Elgamal 2004; Indraratna et al. 2013).
Among various constitutive models (e.g., Duncan and Chang
1970; Fahey and Carter 1993), the Cam clay models have been ef-
fective and convenient at capturing the nonlinear behavior of soft
clay (Mita et al. 2004). Specific application of a modified Cam clay
(MCC) model (Roscoe and Burland 1968) not only quantifies the
void ratio dependency of the soil compressibility, but has also been
successful in capturing the cyclic loading–induced complex behav-
ior of soft ground (Carter et al. 1982; Ni 2012; Ni et al. 2014).
In this paper, the numerical model developed previously by the
authors (Indraratna et al. 2013) is significantly extended to predict
the behavior of stone column–reinforced soft clay under both static
and cyclic loadings using the MCC theory.
Numerical Modeling
Problem Identification
A soft clay layer of thickness H overlies an impervious rigid boun-
dary and supports an embankment (height = He, unit weight = γe).
The soft soil is improved by a group of stone columns; the radii
of the column and the unit cell are rc and re, respectively. Consid-
ering the phenomena of smear and clogging, the cross section of the
entire unit cell has four distinct zones [for more details see
Indraratna et al. (2013)]: unclogged column zone, clogged column
zone, smear zone, and the outer undisturbed soil zone. With steady
and uniform load intensity (ws) imposed on the ground surface, the
average load intensity on the ground surface can be expressed
as w0 ¼ ws þ γeHe.

Smear Zone Characteristics
The installation of stone columns introduces significant disturbance
in the soft soil around the columns, called the smear zone, whose
features depend on the installation technique adopted in the field
(Kirsch and Bell 2012). Han and Ye (2002) stated that for a typical
stone column, rs=rc varies from 1.1 to 1.2, and kh=ks is usually on
the order of 10, where rs is the radius of the smear zone, and ks is
the horizontal permeability of soil in the smear zone. In the analysis
of Wang (2009), the value of rs=rc was assumed to be in the range
of 1–2 and kh=ks to be 10. As reported by Walker and Indraratna
(2006), the ratio of rs=rc usually varies between 2 and 3, and the
permeability of soil in the smear zone decreases in a parabolic pat-
tern with radial distance. Weber et al. (2010) conducted centrifuge
model tests for smear zone identification around stone columns.
The test results confirmed the existence of three distinct sections

Clogging of Columns
The pore water flow through the columns is adversely affected by
clogging because of a significant decrease in the column permeabil-
ity near the interface. During column installation, the associated
cavity expansion (Ghandeharioon et al. 2010) results in initial
clogging. During consolidation of soft clay under sustained sur-
charge loading, the significant hydraulic gradient at the column–
soil interface produces clogging (Adalier and Elgamal 2004;
Indraratna et al. 2013). The clogging initiates a reduction in the
effective radius of the column in terms of drainage and reducing
the permeability in the clogged zone.

Development of Computer Program
The entire computation was performed using the finite-difference
method adopting forward, backward, and central difference tech-
niques. For this purpose, a user-friendly program, COLMCC,
was written in Fortran 90. Analysis was performed for the 261 el-
ements with 300 nodes in axisymmetric unit cell and 15,000 nodes
in the space-time frame (nr ¼ 10, nz ¼ 30, and nt ¼ 50), which
ensures a reasonable compromise between computational time
and accuracy.
Validation
To validate the model developed, comparisons of the numerical re-
sults obtained from the MCC model are made with the available
field test results and existing numerical solutions. Oh et al. (2007)
carried out field-based investigations in soft estuarine clays in
Queensland, Australia, with a trial embankment incorporating three
separate sections as follows: two sections with stone columns of 2
and 3 m spacings (square pattern), plus a reference section with no
stone columns. The numerical model is valid for a rigid and imper-
vious unit cell base, which implies an end-bearing column. As re-
ported by Oh et al. (2007), the trial embankment was built along the
deepest section of the very soft to soft organic clay layer, which
extended from the ground surface to a maximum depth of 13.5 m,
overlying the older alluvium (stiff to hard, silty clay). The stone
columns were embedded to a depth of 16 m below the ground
surface. Hence, the installed stone columns are essentially end-
bearing. A comparison of computed ground settlements with the
field test results is presented in Fig. 1(a). The real time has been
normalized as a modified time factor T 0rð¼ kht=mvγwH2Þ (Basack
2010). The computational parameters used here were adopted from
Oh et al. (2007) and are presented in Table 1. The average value of
mv was adopted as the initial coefficient of volume compressibility,
while the horizontal soil permeability kh was estimated from the
average coefficient of consolidation and average mv. As reported
by Oh et al. (2007), the trial embankment was constructed at Gold
Coast, located in the southeast part of Queensland, Australia, which
is approximately 114 km from the Ballina site. At the Queensland
site, the soft estuarine clay was reported to exist up to a depth of
10–13.5 m below the ground surface, while at Ballina, the soft
marine clay was reported to exist up to 10 m below the ground
surface. The shear strength, moisture content, and compression
index of both soils varied in ranges of 5–20 kPa, 80–100%, and
0.3–0.5, respectively (Oh et al. 2007; Indraratna 2010). Because
of this similarity, the values of the MCC model parameters adopted
for validation were chosen in ranges of 2.5 ≤e0 ≤3.5 and
0.5 ≤λ ≤0.6; the values of the relevant soil parameters at the Bal-
lina site were reported to be e0 ¼ 2.9 and λ ¼ 0.57 (Indraratna
2010). In comparison with the previously developed linear model
(Indraratna et al. 2013), the numerical results by the MCC model
related to the soil parameters e0 ¼ 2.5 and λ ¼ 0.5 are observed to
be close enough to the field data, particularly in the range of
0.002 < T 0r < 0.006, with an average deviation of approximately
5.7%. Thus, the current model provides better predictions in
comparison to the previously developed linear model.
The Malaysian Highway Authority constructed a number of test
embankments on Muar Plain, Malaysia (Redana 1999), using dif-
ferent ground improvement techniques, including sand compaction
piles (SCPs). At the site, soft clay was reported to exist up to a
depth of 20 m below the ground surface in three distinct layers
of overlying dense, silty sand. The piles were placed in a square
grid pattern at 2.2 m spacing. The embankment was constructed
in three stages with rest periods [Fig. 1(b)], and the settlements
were monitored and recorded for 550 days. Redana (1999) con-
ducted a finite-element analysis for a single SCP to compare with
the field settlements. The SCP, possessing greater stiffness com-
pared to the surrounding soft soil, is likely to behave as stone col-
umns from the viewpoint of a load transfer mechanism. Therefore,
the field observations and the finite-element results are compared
with the present MCC model, with the soil and pile parameters
being estimated as the weighted average and ignoring the drainage
through the base layer. The steady-state concentration ratio ns was
taken to be 4.7 (Basack 2010). Analysis was carried out with and
without clogging (for clogging, α ¼ 0.8 and αk ¼ 1.0). The input
parameters chosen for computations taken from Redana (1999) are
presented in Table 1. The average values of kh and mv used in the
finite-element analysis by Redana (1999) were adopted in the
present study. The comparison of time pattern of variation in
ground settlement is portrayed in Fig. 1(b). Compared to the
finite-element results (Redana 1999) and those obtained using
the previously developed linear model (Indraratna et al. 2013),
the MCC model is observed to be close to the field observations
for time ranges of 0 ≤t ≤200 days and 300 ≤t ≤450 days.
With the chosen clogging parameters, the predicted settlements
are observed to be even closer to the field measurements compared
to the linear model, with an average variation of approximately
10.3%. Deviations from the field observations were observed

based on the MCC model essentially depends on the appropriate

is presented below.

The behavior of a stone column–reinforced soft ground under cyclic

Blewett and Woodward 2001), application of the MCC theory has
been found to be appropriate in predicting the reinforced soft soil
(Carteretal. 1982;Ni2012;Nietal.2014) responseundercyclicload.

Cyclic Results and Interpretations
The cyclic model described previously has been validated with
available numerical and experimental results. Ni (2012) developed
a numerical scheme to analyze the pore pressure buildup in soft
clay for cyclic stress pulses using prefabricated vertical drains
(PVDs) and validated the same with laboratory test data of
Indraratna et al. (2009a) (Table 3). The MCC model can be utilized
for PVDs as well if the stress concentration ratio is set equal to
unity (Indraratna et al. 2013). A comparison of the computed
and experimental data is depicted in Fig. 6(a). As observed, the
predicted values of excess pore water pressure obtained using
the present model are closer to the experimental values compared
to those obtained by the analysis of Ni (2012). The introduction
of the in situ earth pressure coefficient K0 and the adoption of
depthwise average values of effective stresses have improved the
present numerical results.
Raju et al. (2013) conducted a series of cyclic plate load tests on
single end-bearing columns in black cotton soil at Karnataka, India.
For each load cycle, the peak cyclic stress was applied and main-
tained till the settlement plate was steady; the load was then fully
released to study the rebound. The variation in the observed
residual soft soil settlement against the applied peak load intensity
was compared with those obtained using the present MCC model
[Fig. 6(b)]. The peak stress was normalized by GγwH, while the
residual settlemnt was nondimensionalized by H, where G is the
specific gravity of soft soil particles and H is the initial height
of the soft clay bed. To simulate the cyclic loading pattern in
the numerical model, the applied vertical stress was increased step-
wise at a rate of 10 kPa till the peak values were attained. Most of
the input soil and column data were obtained from Raju et al. (2012,
2013), with few assumed soil parameters (Table 3). The residual soil
settlements were computed with values of e0, λ, and κ using the
e −ln p 0 correlations. As observed, the residual settlement in-
creased following a parabolic pattern with ascending slope. The
average deviation between the computed results and test data was
found to be less than 10%, which validates the proposed model.

cyclic loading parameters from Ni et al. (2014) were used. Analysis

of p 0 and q. However, for lower values of the cyclic loading param-

increasing value of ns induces a higher stress in the column, thereby
decreasing the stress transfer to the soil, which ultimately
reduces the effective stress p 0. This observation implies that under

The present model and the equations derived are based on the
assumption that compressive strains within the soil mass and the
stone column occur only in the vertical direction, which inevitably
implies a K0-condition to be represented in the unit cell. This sim-
plified assumption has been successfully applied to several existing
solutions (Han and Ye 2001, 2002; Wang 2009; Indraratna et al.
2013). The authors do agree that lateral deformation of columns
under a high embankment load is obvious owing to the limited lat-
eral confinement caused by the surrounding soft soil (Zhang et al.
2013), which initiates a passive state of the soil in the unit cell. It
is true that higher magnitudes of cyclic loading (amplitude, fre-
quency, and number of cycles) are likely to induce a significant
buildup of excess pore water pressure in soft clay, thereby substan-
tially reducing the column lateral confinement. However, for lower
magnitudes of cyclic loading, the assumed K0-condition in the unit
cell does not seem to affect the numerical results significantly, for
example, the validation (Fig. 7). The proposed model predictions
are satisfactory for lower magnitudes of cyclic loading.
It is true that the volumetric compressibility of soft soil varies
nonlinearly with the void ratio. Although the linear model previ-
ously developed by the authors (Indraratna et al. 2013) was rela-
tively simpler, accurate prediction was possible only with an
appropriate choice of the soft soil compressibility (Indraratna et al.
2005). In the proposed MCC model, on the other hand, only two
additional soft soil parameters, e0 and λ, are required for compu-
tation, and they can be determined using conventional oedometer
tests (Gasparre 2005).
Another major shortcoming of the earlier linear model is its in-
ability to capture the effect of cyclic load. It is already established
that the MCC theory, compared to the other existing solutions, has
been largely successful in predicting soft clay behavior under cyclic
loading that is applicable to transport infrastructure (Carter et al.
1982; Ni et al. 2014). In the proposed model, the yield surface
contraction and resulting buildup of cyclic excess pore water pres-
sure has been captured to provide more realistic predictions.
Model Limitations
Although the MCC model captures the performance of stone
column–reinforced soft ground under static and cyclic loadings,
the model is unable to capture the load-settlement characteristics
of a partially penetrated column which produce a vertical compo-
nent of pore water flow in the soft soil (Han and Ye 2001). Also, at
higher magnitudes of loading (static and cyclic), the effect of ro-
tation of the principal stress axes due to obvious shear stresses in-
duced in the soil and the column–soil interface (Indraratna et al.
2013) is not captured by the MCC model. The effect of degradation
in soft soil strength and stiffness initiated by cyclic stress (Basack
and Dey 2012) is not included in the proposed model.
Conclusions
A numerical solution for predicting the performance of stone
column–reinforced soft ground supporting transport infrastructures
has been developed based on the MCC model. The solution adopts
a free-strain hypothesis under unit cell conditions. A comparison of
the numerical results with the available field data and previous
theoretical studies validates the proposed model. It indicates that
adoption of nonlinear variation of soft soil compressibility against
its void ratio as per the MCC model initiates a retarding effect on
the consolidation characteristics compared to the conventional lin-
ear model, with the deviation being as high as 30–40%. Compared
to the excess pore water pressure, the values of ground settlement
were observed to be more affected by the variation in the model
parameters e0 and λ. The ground settlement, having a direct cor-
relation with the parameter mv, is highly sensitive to these param-
eters. An asymptotic stabilizing tendency of the time pattern of
variation in ground settlement was found, except for the initial time
(T 0r < 0.001), where a slight reverse curvature is noted. With in-
creasing values of the initial void ratio e0 of the soft clay, both
the excess pore water pressure and settlement increase following
a curvilinear pattern with ascending slopes. Conversely, increasing
values of the slope λ of the e −ln p 0 curve produced a curvilinear
decrease in the values of the average excess pore water pressure
and ground settlement with descending slopes. The rate of change
in the slope of these curves has been observed to increase with the
value of T 0r.
Under cyclic loading, the excess pore water pressure is observed
to build up with the number of cycles, with a gradual stabilizing
tendency. The increase in both the amplitude and the frequency
of cyclic loading produces an accelerating effect on the generation
of excess pore water pressure. However, for higher cyclic loads, the
model is unable to capture the true response possibly owing to the
rotation of the principal axes induced by the significant shear stress
components. The generation of cyclic excess pore water pressure
decreases asymptotically with increasing values of the steady-stress
concentration ratio ns, the rate of reduction being quite sharp for
1 < ns < 3. Under identical cyclic loading parameters, a smaller
excess pore water pressure was developed for stone column–
reinforced soft ground compared to PVDs.
It is evident that in contrast to the possible convenience of
the current nonlinear approach, the accuracy of the predicted
consolidation and settlement characteristics of reinforced soft
ground is possible only with an appropriate choice of MCC model
parameters.
